\section*{Abstract}
Closed-Circuit Television (CCTV) cameras are widely used for maintaining security, discouraging unlawful activities, and assisting in investigations. However, conventional systems depend heavily on manual monitoring of large video streams, which often leads to missed incidents due to human error. To address this issue, there is a growing need to shift from passive monitoring towards intelligent, automated systems capable of processing both video and audio in real time. This project focuses on building an AI-based anomaly detection framework that integrates computer vision and sound analysis to identify unusual activities and security threats as they occur. By combining detection with an automated alerting mechanism, the system reduces reliance on human supervision and enhances response time. Using comprehensive training datasets and model-driven learning, the system demonstrates how Artificial Intelligence (AI) can transform traditional surveillance into a more scalable, reliable, and efficient solution to ensure safety in a variety of environments.
\pagebreak

\section*{Executive Summary}
CCTV cameras have become a common sight in schools, hospitals, markets, transport hubs, shops, and workplaces. They are installed with the aim of keeping people safe and assisting in investigations when incidents occur. However, the way they are typically used today is far from efficient. The major drawback is the reliance on continuous human monitoring. Watching multiple screens for long hours is monotonous and exhausting, and as a result, important events are often overlooked. Because of this, many incidents such as fights, theft, or even medical emergencies go unnoticed until it is too late.

Our project, Video Surveillance Anomaly Detector, aims to make CCTV monitoring smarter and less dependent on human attention. Instead of serving only as a recording tool, the system actively observes and listens in real time by using computer vision and audio analysis. Whenever it detects unusual activities—such as screaming, fighting, fainting, or loud noises like gunshots or breaking glass—it immediately generates an alert. This ensures that security personnel are only notified when an actual issue arises, reducing unnecessary screen-watching.

The system is designed to detect suspicious behavior including unauthorized access, while also identifying audio cues that some serious event might have occurred such as screams, alarms, breaking glass, or gunshots. It further recognizes sudden body posture changes, for example when someone faints or collapses, and provides real-time alerts for faster response. In addition, it offers basic analytics such as heatmaps that highlight crowded or high-activity areas.

The current scope of this project is to build a working prototype with these features. It does not yet include large-scale deployment, forensic-level investigation, or integration with external databases. These advanced enhancements may be considered in the future. For now, the goal is to demonstrate how AI can make CCTV systems more effective in everyday environments.

From our research, we noticed that most existing approaches focus on a single element. Some analyze only video, others look at posture or audio, and a few combine two modalities but usually for traffic-related applications. Very few attempt to integrate video, audio, posture, and contextual signals into one complete system. This is where our project stands out. By merging these factors, we aim to create a more practical and reliable solution—especially for Pakistani schools, hospitals, and markets, where crowds are common and manual monitoring is often ineffective.

The potential impact of this system is significant. For security staff, it reduces fatigue and allows them to respond only when necessary. For institutions, it improves safety while saving resources otherwise spent on continuous human monitoring. Over time, systems like this could help lower crime rates, improve emergency handling, and even reduce operational costs.

In short, the Video Surveillance Anomaly Detector shifts CCTV from being a passive recording tool to an active security partner. It not only captures footage but also understands when something unusual is happening and reports it instantly. This project highlights how AI can be applied in a practical, everyday manner to improve safety and make surveillance more effective.

\chapter{Introduction}
CCTV systems have become an essential part of modern security. They are widely installed in streets, offices, schools, hospitals, and transport hubs with the purpose of deterring crime, monitoring activities, and providing evidence when incidents occur. However, in practice, these systems depend heavily on human operators who must continuously monitor multiple screens. This task is both exhausting and prone to error, which often results in delayed detection of serious events such as theft or accidents. As the number of cameras grows, it becomes increasingly difficult to rely only on manual supervision.

With recent progress in AI, surveillance technology can now move beyond passive recording. Through computer vision, systems can analyze posture, movement, and crowd behavior, while audio analysis can detect critical sounds such as screams, alarms, or glass breaking. By combining video and audio, surveillance can become an active system that automatically identifies abnormal situations and generates alerts in real time. This not only improves accuracy but also reduces the dependence on continuous human attention.

The motivation behind this project is to design an AI-based anomaly detection system that enhances the capabilities of traditional CCTV. The system aims to recognize unusual human activities, detect suspicious sounds, and adapt to different environments such as crowded markets or sensitive zones. In doing so, it provides faster alerts, supports decision-making for security teams, and contributes to safer public and private spaces.

Traditional surveillance systems are highly dependent on manual monitoring. They are costly to maintain, error-prone, and not effective for large-scale environments. \section{Problem Elaboration}
To better understand how the proposed anomaly detection system will work, the whole solution can be broken down into smaller modules. Each module has its own role, from collecting the right kind of data to finally showing alerts to the security staff. Explaining it this way makes it easier to design, test, and later implement in a real environment.
\subsection{Data Collection}
The very first step is collecting suitable data. A model cannot learn on its own, it learns only from examples. So both normal and abnormal activities need to be gathered. Normal data may include walking, sitting, vehicles moving in a straight path, or people talking. Abnormal samples will be the opposite – theft, fights, accidents, or even health issues like fainting. To make the system reliable, data should be collected under different situations like day and night, indoor and outdoor, crowded places such as markets and less busy areas such as corridors, and also in different weather conditions including fog and rain. This way, the model will not fail when it faces real-world variations.
\subsection{Data Pre-processing}
The raw data is usually messy and not ready for direct use. Cleaning and pre-processing will be done to make it usable. For video, important frames will be extracted, resized to the same size, and noise will be reduced. Unwanted background or useless footage will be removed.
For images, segmentation will be applied to separate objects, and labeling will be done to tag them as normal or abnormal. Augmentation like rotation, flipping, or changing brightness will be applied to increase variety. This step makes the dataset stronger and more balanced.
\subsection{Feature Extraction}
After pre-processing, the system needs to understand what to look at in the data. This is done through feature extraction. Using deep learning methods such as Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), the system will automatically pick up important features from frames. These may include shapes, human postures, sudden movements, or objects like knives or helmets. Learning these features helps the system separate regular events from suspicious ones.
\subsection{Model Training and Evaluation}
Once features are ready, the system will be trained. The training process teaches the model how to identify the difference between normal and abnormal activities. Various algorithms will be tested, and the one with better performance will be chosen.
The model will also be evaluated on unseen data to check its accuracy and response time. This step is important because a system that is not tested well may give false alarms or miss real threats.
\subsection{Anomaly Detection}
This module is the main brain of the system. When a new activity is observed, the system compares it with learned patterns. If it matches normal behavior, no action is taken. But if the pattern looks unusual – like someone falling suddenly or a fight starting – it will be flagged as suspicious. Quick classification at this stage ensures that security staff can act without delays.
\subsection{Alert and Reporting}
After detection, the system must immediately inform the right people. Real-time alerts will be sent through a simple desktop interface. These alerts will contain details such as the time, date, and type of anomaly. Along with alerts, a log file will be created for record keeping.
A reporting interface will also be included where administrators can check past cases, study patterns, and improve overall safety measures.
\subsection{System Integration and User Interface}
Finally, all modules need to work together in a single setup. A user-friendly interface will be designed where live video feeds, alerts, and reports can be accessed easily. The design will be kept simple so that even non-technical security staff can use it without difficulty. Integration ensures that the technical side (AI modules) and the human side (end-users) are connected properly.

In short, the problem is broken into steps: collecting data, cleaning it, extracting features, training the model, detecting anomalies, sending alerts, and finally showing everything in an easy interface. Each step is important and supports the next one. Together, they will form a smart surveillance system that reduces human errors, gives timely alerts, and helps in preventing or responding to incidents more effectively.

\section{Detailed Literature Review}
This section presents the detailed literature review of the studies related to our project.

\subsection{Cloze Test Helps: Effective Video Anomaly Detection via Learning to Complete Video Events (2020)}
This paper presents a novel cloze-test based approach to video anomaly detection. The main idea is to make the model predict missing parts of a video sequence and compare them with the actual frames. Any significant deviation between the predicted and real sequence indicates an abnormal event.
\subsubsection{Summary of the Research Item}
Yu et al. proposed a method inspired by language cloze tests, but applied to video data. The model learns both spatial and temporal patterns by attempting to reconstruct missing segments of a video. When abnormal events occur, the reconstruction error becomes higher, allowing the system to detect anomalies without explicit labeling. This approach was tested on public benchmark datasets and showed improved detection accuracy compared to conventional methods that rely on handcrafted features or simple frame differencing.
\subsubsection{Critical Analysis}
A key strength of this work is its innovative way of combining temporal and spatial reasoning, allowing the model to detect subtle anomalies that may not be obvious in a single frame. It is also well-suited for unsupervised settings. However, the approach is computationally expensive and may not scale well for real-time surveillance systems with limited processing power.
\subsubsection{Relationship to Proposed Work}
This work provides a strong foundation for temporal reasoning in anomaly detection, which is essential for real-time monitoring in complex environments. The proposed system builds on this by integrating not only video analysis but also audio cues and posture-based anomaly detection to improve detection accuracy and robustness in real-world scenarios like hospitals and schools.

\subsection{Generative Cooperative Learning for Unsupervised Video Anomaly Detection (2022)}
This research explores Generative Cooperative Learning (GCL) for video anomaly detection. Unlike traditional supervised approaches, this model works in an unsupervised manner by making a generator and discriminator cooperate to identify anomalies.
\subsubsection{Summary of the Research Item}
Zaheer et al. introduced a cooperative framework where the generator creates normal event representations while the discriminator evaluates them. By learning the distribution of normal patterns, the model identifies any deviation as an anomaly. This reduces the need for labeled data, which is often scarce in real-world surveillance applications. The system was evaluated on multiple datasets and showed state-of-the-art performance in unsupervised anomaly detection.
\subsubsection{Critical Analysis}
The major strength of this work is its ability to function without labeled data while maintaining stability in training, which is often a challenge with adversarial networks. Its performance in detecting abnormal events in crowded environments is commendable. However, it requires a large amount of normal data for robust training, and the model’s adaptability in dynamic environments is limited.
\subsubsection{Relationship to Proposed Work}
The unsupervised nature of this work is highly relevant to the proposed system, which also aims to minimize reliance on labeled datasets. While Zaheer et al. focus on temporal anomaly detection through cooperative learning, our work extends their approach by incorporating additional input modalities such as posture analysis and audio cues to make the system more context-aware.

\subsection{Exploiting Spatial-Temporal Correlations for Video Anomaly Detection (2022)}
This study focuses on exploiting spatial-temporal correlations to improve video anomaly detection. It highlights the importance of analyzing object behavior across both space and time to identify unusual activities more effectively.
\subsubsection{Summary of the Research Item}
Zhao et al. developed a method that captures both spatial and temporal patterns in surveillance videos. By tracking object movements and behavioral changes over time, the system identifies deviations from learned normal patterns. Their approach proved effective in crowded scenes, where anomalies often manifest through motion irregularities rather than static appearance.
\subsubsection{Critical Analysis}
A major strength of this work is its ability to jointly model spatial and temporal dependencies, improving detection accuracy in real-world, crowded scenarios. However, the method demands significant computational resources and may face difficulties in low-activity environments where patterns are less defined.
\subsubsection{Relationship to Proposed Work}
The focus on spatial-temporal reasoning closely aligns with the architecture of the proposed system. Our project builds on this foundation by adding posture analysis and zone sensitivity, which enables context-based anomaly detection rather than relying only on motion irregularities.

\subsection{Video Abnormal Event Detection by Learning to Complete Visual Cloze Tests (2020)}
This work addresses abnormal event detection and temporal localization in surveillance videos, focusing on identifying when and where anomalies occur within a clip. It aims to provide interpretable event localization as well as detection.
\subsubsection{Summary of the Research Item}
Zhu et al. proposed a framework to detect and localize abnormal events across both space and time in surveillance footage. Their approach couples temporal localization with event-level detection, so detected anomalies are given explicit start/end times and spatial regions. The model was evaluated on CCTV-style datasets commonly used in anomaly detection research and demonstrated improvements in temporal localization accuracy compared to baseline reconstruction- or prediction-based methods.
\subsubsection{Critical Analysis}
A primary strength of this study is its focus on localization, which increases the practical usefulness of detections for forensic review and operator response. It also offers interpretability by indicating the temporal extent of events. Weaknesses include reliance on well-annotated datasets for evaluation and limited robustness to domain shifts (e.g., new scene layouts or camera angles), which may reduce generalization in heterogeneous deployment settings.
\subsubsection{Relationship to Proposed Work}
This research informs our goal of producing actionable alerts (with short clips and timestamps). We adopt their emphasis on localization but extend it by fusing posture cues and audio, and by using zone-based thresholds to reduce false positives in different scene types.

\subsection{Automatic pose recognition for dangerous situations in Ambient-Assisted Living (2020)}
Guerra et al. investigated pose-based automatic recognition systems to monitor risky situations, particularly aimed at assisted living scenarios where fall detection and abnormal posture recognition are critical.
\subsubsection{Summary of the Research Item}
The authors developed a skeleton-based pose recognition system that tracks joint positions over time to detect falls and other dangerous postures. Their pipeline uses pose-estimation to obtain skeletal keypoints, followed by rule-based and learned classifiers to decide if a pose is risky. The approach was validated in controlled environments typical for Ambient Assisted Living (AAL) applications and demonstrated high detection rates for falls.
\subsubsection{Critical Analysis}
Strengths include interpretability and relatively low computational cost when pose-estimation is efficient; skeleton features are robust to clothing and color variation. Weaknesses arise in crowded or highly occluded scenes where full skeletons are not visible, and in non-standard camera placements common in public surveillance, limiting transfer to large-scale CCTV. Also, most tests were in controlled residential settings rather than unconstrained public environments.
\subsubsection{Relationship to Proposed Work}
Guerra et al.’s pose-based insights are directly applicable to our posture/behavior module (lying down, fainting, crawling). We extend their approach by integrating pose information with context (zone sensitivity) and complementary visual/audio cues to handle occlusion and crowded public spaces.

\subsection{Audio-Visual Dataset and Method for Anomaly Detection in Traffic Videos (MAVAD) (2023)}
Leporowski et al. introduced a multimodal dataset and method (MAVAD) that fuses audio and visual streams for traffic anomaly detection, demonstrating the benefits of audio-visual integration.
\subsubsection{Summary of the Research Item}
This paper released MAVAD, a dataset of traffic scenes with synchronized audio and video, and proposed an audio-visual fusion method based on cross-attention (AVACA). The authors showed multimodal fusion improves detection of events like collisions, honking-induced incidents, and other anomalies where audio provides complementary evidence to visuals. They evaluated robustness across differing illumination and weather conditions.
\subsubsection{Critical Analysis}
The dataset and method strongly support the case for multimodal fusion; audio often disambiguates visual uncertainties (e.g., occluded collisions). However, MAVAD is traffic-specific, so developing models trained on it may not generalize directly to indoor or human-centric scenes. The proposed fusion method also adds model complexity and computational cost.
\subsubsection{Relationship to Proposed Work}
MAVAD motivates our decision to fuse audio and video — especially for events with characteristic sounds (gunshots, glass breaking, or screams). We plan to adapt fusion architectures like cross-attention to human-centric domains and incorporate zone-specific models for different scene types (indoor vs outdoor).

\subsection{Feature-trajectory smoothed high-speed model (2023)}
Sun et al. proposed a high-speed anomaly detection framework that smooths feature trajectories to reduce noise and false positives, targeting real-time deployment on large camera networks.
\subsubsection{Summary of the Research Item}
The authors designed a pipeline that smooths the temporal trajectory of deep features extracted from video frames, which stabilizes per-frame anomaly scores and reduces spurious detections. The approach emphasizes computational efficiency and was benchmarked for real-time performance and scalability across multiple camera feeds.
\subsubsection{Critical Analysis}
Key strengths are speed and robustness to transient noise (camera jitter, short occlusions). This makes the approach practical for operational systems. The trade-off is that trajectory smoothing can sometimes dampen short-lived but important anomalies (e.g., sudden fainting or quick aggressive movements), potentially increasing false negatives for brief events.
\subsubsection{Relationship to Proposed Work}
We adopt similar smoothing and efficiency strategies for our real-time alert system, but compensate for potential sensitivity loss by combining trajectory smoothing with pose-based indicators and short-window audio checks so brief but critical events remain detectable.

\subsection{Center-Guided Discriminative Learning (2021)}
Wan et al. presented a weakly supervised framework for anomaly detection using multiple-instance learning (MIL) guided by a center loss to cluster normal examples more tightly.
\subsubsection{Summary of the Research Item}
This paper formulates anomaly detection under weak supervision: videos are labeled only at the clip or video level, not at frame-level. The method uses MIL to separate anomalous clips from normal ones along with a center-guided loss that pulls normal features closer together and pushes anomalies away. This reduces annotation cost while maintaining competitive detection performance on benchmark datasets.
\subsubsection{Critical Analysis}
The approach’s strength is practicality: it significantly cuts annotation effort while producing good detection scores. However, MIL-based systems can suffer from coarse temporal resolution (hard to localize frame-level anomalies accurately) and are sensitive to the way clips are packaged into “bags”. Performance may degrade when anomalies are sparse or subtle within long videos.
\subsubsection{Relationship to Proposed Work}
Weak supervision is attractive because annotating posture + audio events across many CCTV feeds is expensive. We plan to incorporate MIL-inspired training for components (e.g., zone-level models) while using selective frame-level labels or pose heuristics to improve localization where needed.

\subsection{MC-MIL (Multi-Camera MIL) (2023)}
Pereira and Maia proposed MC-MIL, a multi-camera multiple-instance learning approach that leverages synchronized views to overcome occlusion and enrich anomaly evidence.
\subsubsection{Summary of the Research Item}
The MC-MIL method aggregates information from multiple camera views observing overlapping regions. Using MIL to handle weak labels, the framework leverages complementary view information to detect anomalies more robustly despite occlusion or viewpoint limitations. Experiments on multi-camera datasets demonstrated gains over single-view MIL approaches.
\subsubsection{Critical Analysis}
Multi-view fusion markedly improves robustness in crowded or occluded scenes and is a strong fit for multi-camera installations (airports, malls). Downsides include the need for precise temporal synchronization and higher computational and networking requirements to gather and process multi-view streams in real time.
\subsubsection{Relationship to Proposed Work}
Although our prototype may initially target single-camera setups, MC-MIL highlights how zone-based logic can be extended to multi-camera deployments in future work. Zone-based sensitivity in our design can later incorporate multi-view fusion for critical zones (e.g., restricted entry points).

\subsection{An Investigation of Videos for Abnormal Behavior Detection (2023)}
This paper investigates abnormal behavior detection in real-world CCTV recordings using deep learning techniques. The authors emphasize the importance of developing cost-efficient and real-time systems capable of recognizing suspicious activities like violence, property damage, loitering, and crowding events in public surveillance environments.
\subsubsection{Summary of the Research Item}
The authors developed a framework that extracts spatial-temporal features from surveillance videos using a combination of CNN-based feature extractors and lightweight classifiers. They experimented with both standard datasets (like UCSD Ped2 and Avenue) and real-world CCTV recordings. The system detects abnormal events such as fights, theft, vandalism, or suspicious lingering based on deviations from learned normal patterns. Their focus was on making the model efficient and deployable in real-time, especially for public safety and law enforcement scenarios.
\subsubsection{Critical Analysis}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Strengths: \begin{itemize}
        \item Uses real-world scenarios beyond controlled datasets, making the research more practical.
        \item Focuses on lightweight model design suitable for real-time deployments.
        \item Covers a wide range of abnormal behaviors instead of focusing on a single category.
    \end{itemize}
    \item Weaknesses: \begin{itemize}
        \item The approach lacks advanced contextual awareness like zone sensitivity or adaptive thresholds.
        \item The accuracy of anomaly detection decreases in complex lighting and crowded scenes.
        \item Audio signals are not considered, which could improve detection robustness.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
\subsubsection{Relationship to Proposed Work}
This paper aligns well with the core objectives of the proposed project — identifying unusual behaviors in surveillance video in real-time. However, while their approach focuses purely on visual analysis, our system integrates both audio and video anomaly detection along with posture analysis and zone-based sensitivity. This makes our approach more comprehensive for environments like hospitals, universities, and markets in Pakistan. Moreover, their lightweight approach offers useful insights for keeping the system computationally efficient.

\subsection{Deep Learning-Based Anomaly Detection in Real-Time Video (2024)}
This paper presents a balanced architecture combining I3D-ResNet50 features with MIL to achieve real-time anomaly detection with good accuracy/speed trade-offs.
\subsubsection{Summary of the Research Item}
The study integrates a strong spatio-temporal feature extractor (I3D-ResNet50) with a MIL-based decision head optimized for streaming video. The authors focused on pipeline optimizations to reduce latency and demonstrated near-real-time inference on moderately powerful hardware, while retaining high detection accuracy across common surveillance datasets.
\subsubsection{Critical Analysis}
Strengths include pragmatic attention to latency and an engineering focus on making high-performing models practical for live operation. Weaknesses include the dependency on relatively capable hardware and potential difficulties transferring to low-cost edge cameras or microcontrollers without further optimization.
\subsubsection{Relationship to Proposed Work}
This paper provides concrete engineering lessons for our real-time alerting module — how to structure the pipeline and which parts to prioritize for optimization (e.g., feature extraction vs. classifier). We will use these insights for deployment planning and benchmark targets.

\subsection{Efficient Human Violence Recognition for Surveillance in Real Time (2024)}
This research presents a real-time violence recognition framework tailored for public surveillance systems. The authors address the challenge of detecting violent actions accurately and quickly, with the ultimate goal of enabling early response in public spaces such as streets, schools, and transportation hubs.
\subsubsection{Summary of the Research Item}
The paper introduces a deep learning model optimized for real-time processing, combining MobileNet for efficient feature extraction and a temporal CNN for action recognition. The system identifies violent activities such as fighting, pushing, or chaotic movements within short video segments. They validated the approach on benchmark violence datasets like Hockey Fight and RWF-2000, achieving competitive accuracy while maintaining low latency. The model’s design emphasizes deployment feasibility on edge devices and low-power setups.
\subsubsection{Critical Analysis}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Strengths: \begin{itemize}
        \item Strong emphasis on real-time performance and resource efficiency.
        \item Demonstrated effectiveness in detecting fast, aggressive actions in crowded environments.
        \item Uses lightweight models that can be integrated into practical surveillance systems.
    \end{itemize}
    \item Weaknesses: \begin{itemize}
        \item Limited focus on non-violent anomalies (e.g., medical emergencies, suspicious objects).
        \item System relies entirely on visual cues and does not leverage audio or contextual metadata.
        \item Requires a relatively clean view of human movement, which can be challenging in cluttered environments.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
\subsubsection{Relationship to Proposed Work}
This work directly supports the violence and aggression detection module of the proposed project. While the authors concentrate solely on violence detection, our system expands this scope to include posture-based anomalies, suspicious objects, unauthorized entry, and audio anomalies. Additionally, their model’s real-time focus aligns with our objective to build a scalable, low-latency system suitable for real-world Pakistani settings. We can draw from their lightweight architecture to make our system efficient and deployable on modest hardware.

\section{Conclusion}
From the reviewed literature, it is evident that video surveillance anomaly detection has rapidly evolved with deep learning and multimodal approaches. Earlier works primarily focused on single-domain problems like posture detection, object recognition, or video-only anomalies. More recent research emphasizes fusion of modalities, weak supervision, and context-aware adaptability, but still lacks integrated solutions that combine video, audio, posture, environmental awareness, and zone-based control in a unified system.

The proposed work builds on these advances by designing an AI-powered real-time anomaly detection system that addresses these gaps through multimodal fusion, adaptive sensitivity, posture recognition, and dynamic zone management—thereby creating a practical, scalable, and intelligent surveillance solution suitable for real-world environments such as hospitals, campuses, and public spaces.

\subsection{Notify Alerts}
Following use case highlights the steps followed to automatically notify security officers when anomalies are detected.

This use case ensures immediate notification of security personnel when anomalies are detected, enabling rapid response to potential security incidents. The notification system operates in real-time, automatically alerting officers through multiple channels including dashboard notifications, email alerts, and optional SMS messages for critical incidents. The system categorizes alerts by severity level (low, medium, high, critical) and routes notifications accordingly. Critical alerts receive immediate attention with priority notification channels, while lower severity alerts are queued for regular review. The notification system includes intelligent filtering to prevent alert fatigue by consolidating similar incidents and prioritizing unique or escalating situations. Officers can customize notification preferences including preferred channels, quiet hours, and severity thresholds. The system maintains notification logs for audit purposes and provides delivery confirmation to ensure alerts reach the intended recipients. In case of network connectivity issues, the system queues notifications and delivers them once connectivity is restored, ensuring no critical alerts are missed.

\section{ Risk Analysis}
Some of the technical and operational risks that may arise during the development and deployment of the system are explained in this section.
\subsection{Model Accuracy and False Positives}
AI models may incorrectly classify normal activities as anomalies, leading to false alerts and reducing system reliability.
\subsection{Environmental Variations}
Factors like poor lighting, shadows, or weather changes can impact detection accuracy and require model adjustments.
\subsection{Audio Analysis Reliability}
External noise interference or poor microphone quality can reduce the effectiveness of audio anomaly detection.
\subsection{Hardware Limitations}
Limited GPU or processing power can affect real-time performance, especially when handling multiple camera feeds simultaneously.

\chapter{Proposed Approach and Methodology}

\section{Video Analysis Layer}

    \item Suspicious Object Detection
    \item Crowd and Entry Monitoring
    \item Environmental Adaptability

\section{System Overview}
Our system uses artificial intelligence and computer vision to automatically detect unusual activities in CCTV videos. Instead of watching long camera footage manually, our system processes recorded videos and identifies abnormal events such as fainting, fighting, or suspicious movements. The system combines video and audio analysis to make better decisions and provide alerts for critical events.

The software is divided into several main modules, each handling a specific task in the anomaly detection process.

\subsection{Video Analysis Module}
This module uses computer vision techniques to process the uploaded CCTV video. It reads video frames, detects motion, tracks people or objects, and analyzes body movements. If something unusual is found, such as sudden falls, crowding, or aggressive behavior, it sends the information to the anomaly detection module for further checking.

\subsection{Audio Analysis Module}
This module focuses on sound-based detection. It extracts and studies sound features from the video to detect loud or abnormal noises such as gunshots, screams, or breaking glass. These sound features are also shared with the anomaly detection module to improve the accuracy of alerts.

\subsection{Anomaly Detection Module}
This is the core of the system. It uses trained AI models to combine both video and audio results. It checks whether the detected event is normal or abnormal.
If any anomaly is found, it immediately sends an alert to the application layer to display a warning to the user and store the event in the database.

\subsection{Alert and Report Management Module}
This module manages all alerts and reports created by the system. When an anomaly is detected, it saves the event details such as time, type, and location. It also allows the user to view past records, statistics, and summaries through a user-friendly dashboard.

\section{System Architecture}
This section provides an overview of our system architecture. Our system follows a layered architecture, offering several advantages such as better abstraction, low coupling between modules, easier maintenance, and flexibility for future improvements.

\begin{figure} [!h]
    \centering
\includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{Figures/System Architecture Diagram.png}
    \caption{System Architecture Diagram}
    \textit{Shows a high-level overview of the system’s architecture.}
    \label{fig:1}
    \
\end{figure}

As shown in the diagram, our system adopts a layered architecture approach to separate different functionalities of the Video Surveillance Anomaly Detector. The system is divided into five distinct layers: the Presentation Layer, which handles the user interface and provides an easy way for security staff or administrators to interact with the system; the Application Layer, which acts as a controller and manages communication between the interface and backend; the Business Layer, which performs the main AI-based anomaly detection using video and audio analysis modules; the Persistence Layer, which manages data access and ensures consistent communication with storage; and finally, the Data Layer, which stores all datasets, AI models, and alert logs for long-term use.

\subsection{Subsystem Architecture}
Architecture of the main subsystems of our system is presented in this section. All modules are implemented in Python, and TensorFlow, OpenCV, and Librosa are used for deep learning, computer vision, and audio feature extraction respectively. These subsystems work together to process uploaded CCTV footage and automatically detect abnormal visual or sound patterns.

\subsubsection{Video Analyzer Subsystem}
This subsystem is responsible for analyzing the visual component of uploaded CCTV recordings to extract motion and behavioral patterns. The architecture of this module is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:2}.

\begin{figure} [!h]
    \centering
\includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{Figures/Sub-system Architecture - Video.png}
    \caption{Architecture of Video Analyzer}
    \textit{Shows the flow of video processing and feature extraction.}
    \label{fig:2}
\end{figure}

The module reads the uploaded CCTV video using OpenCV, extracts individual frames, and performs preprocessing such as resizing, grayscale conversion, and noise reduction. Motion detection is then carried out using frame differencing or background subtraction, followed by object tracking to maintain movement continuity across frames. Visual features like object posture, movement, and velocity are extracted and encoded into numerical vectors. These encoded visual feature vectors are finally passed to the Anomaly Detector Subsystem for further analysis.

\subsubsection{Audio Analyzer Subsystem}
This subsystem focuses on analyzing the sound component of the CCTV recording to detect unusual or alarming noises. The architecture is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:3}.

\begin{figure} [!h]
    \centering
\includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{Figures/Sub-system Architecture - Audio.png}
    \caption{Architecture of Audio Analyzer}
    \textit{Shows the process of audio pre-processing and feature extraction.}
    \label{fig:3}
\end{figure}

The subsystem begins by extracting the audio track from the uploaded CCTV file. After removing background noise and normalizing the sound, Librosa is used to extract relevant features such as MFCC, Spectral Centroid, Chroma, and Zero Crossing Rate. These features are then analyzed for unusual sound intensity or repetition patterns. Finally, the processed descriptors are encoded into numerical feature vectors which are forwarded to the Anomaly Detector Subsystem for final classification.


\subsubsection{Anomaly Detector Subsystem}
This subsystem forms the core intelligence of the system, combining the visual and audio features to identify abnormal activities. Its architecture is presented in Figure~\ref{fig:4}.

\begin{figure} [!h]
    \centering
\includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{Figures/Sub-system Architecture - Anomaly.png}
    \caption{Architecture of Anomaly Detector}
    \textit{Shows the architecture of the multimodal anomaly detection pipeline.}
    \label{fig:4}
\end{figure}

The subsystem receives encoded visual and audio feature vectors from their respective analyzers. Both are merged into a unified multimodal feature set after synchronization and normalization. A deep learning model built using TensorFlow (either CNN or LSTM-based) is then applied to learn spatiotemporal correlations between sound and visual patterns. The model outputs probability scores that are processed by a Decision Module, which classifies each event as Normal or Anomalous. Finally, the Alert and Logging System triggers notifications and stores the anomaly record in the database along with relevant metadata.
